## 1. Market context (four lenses)

### 1.1 AI video (core category)

Dedicated AI video generators and broader “AI video” platforms (generation, editing assistance, avatar video, creative tools) are among the fastest-growing slices of creative AI software. Definitions vary widely by analyst—narrow “AI video generator” vs. broader “AI video” including editing, analytics, and enterprise suites—so ranges must be read as **scopes**, not contradictions.

### 1.2 Creator economy

Creators and small teams are primary consumers of generative video for social, ads, and client work. Demand is fueled by short-form platforms and paid social creative testing. RTAS’s Tester ($5) and Standard ($89/mo) tiers are designed for this buyer’s willingness to pay. *(Tier amounts: VERIFIED product pricing.)*

### 1.3 Generative AI (parent category)

Text, image, audio, and video generation share infrastructure, capital intensity, and trust/regulatory scrutiny. Video is compute-heavier and later to commercialize than text/image, which creates both **opportunity** (greenfield workflows) and **competition** (well-funded model labs).

### 1.4 Enterprise AI

Enterprises adopt generative video more slowly than consumer creators, prioritizing governance, brand safety, procurement, and vendor stability. Avatar/L&D vendors matured enterprise motions earlier; cinematic/open generation vendors are still expanding enterprise packaging. RTAS’s live legal suite and Paddle MoR path support diligence conversations; full enterprise readiness remains a maturity journey (see readiness scores in `PHASE11_SPRINT1_REPORT.md`).

## 5. SWOT-linked market view (summary)

Full SWOT: `company/SWOT.md`.

### Strengths (market-facing)

- Clear commercial packaging and credit economics (VERIFIED tiers)  
- Trust posture suitable for MoR and brand buyers (live legal + AUP framing)  
- Use-case focus on commercials, music videos, animation—not avatar-only  

### Weaknesses (market-facing)

- Early-stage brand awareness vs. Runway/Pika/Synthesia-class recognition  
- Smaller R&D and distribution footprint than frontier labs  
- Enterprise motion not yet at incumbent maturity  

### Opportunities

- Agencies and marketing companies still assembling AI video stacks  
- Second-based pricing literacy rising among buyers  
- Trust differentiation as regulation and payment-partner scrutiny increase  
- Underserved positioning between consumer toys and enterprise avatar suites  

### Threats

- Rapid model commoditization from Big Tech and well-funded startups  
- Price pressure and free tiers from large platforms  
- Policy/platform shifts affecting generative video distribution  
- MoR/domain approval delays impacting conversion
